Key Evacuation Tips

Communication is Key

  • Stay calm and use simple, direct statements; the person you are caring for will respond to the tone you set. Instead of correcting them, use affirmation and reassurance.
  • Know what specifically provides comfort to your loved one, whether it's holding their hand, listening to certain music, or looking at familiar photos.
  • Consider carrying an Alzheimer's Association Companion Card to discreetly inform others that your companion may need extra help and patience.

Plan Their Route & Meeting Place

  • When planning the route, consider how long the person you care for can sit comfortably and how much fuel it will take to get there.
  • Share this evacuation plan with your or the care recipient's support circle, and be sure to include at least one contact who lives outside the disaster area.
  • Choose a safe meeting place outside and far away from their home where everyone can gather after leaving.

Maintain Their Routines & Provide Comfort

  • During the disruption, try to maintain their normal routines as much as possible, especially for eating and medication.
  • Be sure to pack familiar items that will provide comfort to them, such as a favorite blanket, a stuffed toy, or games.

Key Evacuation Tips

Communication is Key

  • Stay calm and think clearly by using simple, direct internal thoughts. The tone you set for yourself can influence your response. Instead of second-guessing, focus on affirmation and reassurance.
  • Know what specifically provides comfort to you in stressful situations, whether it's listening to certain music, having a favorite snack, or reviewing your plan.
  • Keep a card with your emergency contact information and any important medical notes in your wallet or Go-Kit to easily share with first responders if needed.

Plan Your Route & Meeting Place

  • When planning your route, consider how long you can sit comfortably in a car, how much fuel you'll need, and pre-plan for rest stops.
  • Share your evacuation plan with your support circle, and be sure to include at least one contact who lives outside the immediate disaster area.
  • Choose a safe, specific meeting place for your family or support circle to gather after evacuating, located away from your home.

Maintain Your Routines & Provide Comfort

  • During the disruption, try to maintain your normal routines as much as possible, especially for eating, sleeping, and taking any necessary medication.
  • Be sure to pack familiar items that will provide you with comfort or distraction, such as a favorite book, a tablet with downloaded movies, or games.

Key Shelter-in-Place Tips

Prepare Space

  • Designate a safe room or specific space in home to store critical supplies.
  • Gather supplies to last for at least 7 days, including water (at least 7 gallons per person) and non-perishable food that includes comforting and familiar items.
  • Plan for a power loss by having multiple flashlights and/or battery-powered lanterns and extra batteries.

Organize Critical Supplies

  • Keep at least a two-week supply of all necessary medications for both yourself and the person you care for.
  • Have cash on hand in case power outages prevent the use of ATMs or credit cards.
  • Assemble a kit with first-aid, hygiene supplies, and cleaning products, and remember to include pet food and supplies if needed.

Maintain Routines & Provide Comfort

  • As much as possible, try to maintain normal routines for the person you care for, especially for eating, sleeping, and medication.
  • To help create a sense of normalcy, prepare and freeze meal-sized servings of their favorite dishes that can be heated with an outdoor grill or camp stove if you lose power.
  • Plan calming, power-free activities that your loved one enjoys, such as folding laundry, doing puzzles, or playing games.

Key Shelter-in-Place Tips

Prepare Your Space

  • Designate a specific room in your home as a safe room to store your essential items.
  • Gather supplies to last for a minimum of one week. This should include at least 7 gallons of water, non-perishable food, and a manual can opener.
  • Prepare for power outages by having multiple flashlights, battery-powered lanterns, and extra batteries readily available.

Organize Your Critical Supplies

  • Keep at least a two-week supply of all your necessary medications.
  • Have cash on hand in case power outages prevent the use of ATMs or credit cards.
  • Assemble a kit with first-aid, hygiene supplies, and cleaning products. If you have pets, remember their food and supplies as well.

Maintain Your Routines & Comfort

  • Try to maintain your normal routines as much as possible, particularly for meals and taking medication.
  • Prepare and freeze some of your favorite meals ahead of time. These can be heated on an outdoor grill or camp stove if you lose power.
  • Plan for calming, power-free activities you can do, such as reading a book, doing puzzles, or playing games.
